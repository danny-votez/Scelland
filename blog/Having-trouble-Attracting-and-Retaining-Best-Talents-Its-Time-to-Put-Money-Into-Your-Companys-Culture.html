Trouble Attracting and Retaining Best Talents? Do A Culture Investment

Having trouble Attracting and Retaining Best Talents? It's Time to Put Money Into Your Companyâ€™s Culture

You know how difficult it is to attract suitable people if you've tried to hire someone into your organization in the last few months.

You know how difficult it is to locate suitable people if you've tried to hire someone into your company in the last few months. While it's simple to blame the pandemic for the market disruption, this is a problem that will most certainly persist for at least the next decade.

According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, there are now over 10 million job openings. At the same time, the Department of Labor announced that there are 8.7 million potential employees looking for work who are classified as unemployed. As a result, we have a substantial shortage of qualified candidates to fill our open positions. Employers are also noting that the applicants applying have a mismatch of abilities, and that they aren't seeing people who can meet their precise requirements. Baby Boomers are leaving the workforce to enter retirement, compounding an already challenging hiring environment.

This is a barrier for companies attempting to scale and grow. Hiring and retention don't have to be as difficult if these same organizations are ready to take a hard look at their workplace cultures and make changes immediately.

Examine your company's culture to see what's working and what isn't.

We have a propensity to focus on our monthly profit and loss statements and economic markers of success in our businesses, but we must also consider our people and their experiences working for us.

If you care about your customers' experiences with your company, you should equally be concerned about your employees'. Employee experience has a direct impact on customer experience. Your employees will realize their importance to your company if you create a well-designed employee journey. They are well-cared for and are set up for success at every major turning point in their careers.

If your firm hasn't done a culture audit in the last two years, or if you've never done one, it's a good idea to do so to find out what's really going on in your employees' lives. The culture audit can be as basic as asking employees what's working and what isn't, as well as learning more about the issues they face on a regular basis. You can also ask them questions regarding what would cause them to leave your organization if you're feeling very daring.

Now, Who is responsible for this?

The corporate budget rarely includes a line item for culture, but it should. Efforts to change the culture of the company almost always pay off. When you have a supportive workplace culture, employee retention is easier, recruiting and re-recruitment expenses are lower, diversity occurs more naturally, and productivity increases.

Definite Return on Investment for Your Organization

Consider the following article by Shane Mcfeely and Ben Wigert of GALLUP. According to the Authors, a 100-person organization with an average pay of $50,000 may have annual turnover and replacement costs of $660,000 to $2.6 million. It's worth it even if your culture initiatives only save a few employees each year. In the first year, companies that excel in improving their cultures often experience big results.
